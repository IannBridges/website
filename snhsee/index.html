1. General

Generally, the EE booklet is not written for STEM EEs. It is more well suited for historical analysis or English EEs. This, however, should not be discouraging since choosing a slightly more obfuscated EE topic you are interested in rather than one you are not motivated to complete will prove to make the process much, much more enjoyable. You may be reading this document after you have chosen a question, and perhaps after you have been coupled with an advisor. If this is the case, you should know:

It is not too late to change your question!

The EE is usually blown off, however, choosing a meaningful and interesting topic can not only be fun, but incredibly rewarding. Generally, you should be prioritizing your learning during your EE.

Lastly, even though this document is separated into sections, this information can be applied to any EE!

2. Computer Science

"To what extent is Gaussian Elimination more efficient than the Jacobi method at solving systems of linear equations with diagonally dominant coefficient matrices of increasing size in computer systems?"

This was my question. It’s just comparing 2 different strategies of solving a particular type of problem which occurs pretty often in scientific computing, data analysis, and computer graphics. Below are some issues and advice I have to offer:

1.

Understand your question & read the textbook!

The time given to you to "explore" your question is not nearly enough. Multiple times during my exploration I had to question what I actually knew and what my question actually ”Before beginning "research" into your question, read through textbooks (there exists numerous websites dedicated to free textbooks, some of which are questionably legal) to get an accurate outline of what your question actually entails.

2.

Draft your code and paper early.

Although I didn’t intend for it, the bulk amount of my coding was done in the final weeks of my project. Not only was this stressful during college applications, I also had no time to reflect on the results of my experiment and whether or not they made any sense. Creating drafts early, preferably before the start of the school year, prevents this from happening.

3.

Organize before tackling and reevaluate.

Admitedly my paper was defintely research intensive. Although I spent a lot of time reading and researching, I gathered a large set of resources and gained programming skills that will and have benefitted me tremendously. And these aren’t abstract skills like "leadership" and "organization", I use the resources that I procured during this project everyday, and the development of my programming skills and my introduction to the coding community has put me well beyond what any class at PA could’ve provided for me.

I believe that all students planning on doing a Computer Science EE consult IBCSEEWorld for a plethora of submitted CS EEs with grades.

3. Biology

"How accurate is the synthetic rFC compared to the naturally occurring LAL in endotoxin detection?"

This was a comparative analysis of 3 articles (experiments) so it is a little bit unusual, but arguably easier to get into than a full blown biology experiment. This makes it great for those who are intimidated by doing an experiment by entirely by themselves (excluding help from an advisor, of course) for the first time.

1.

Don’t wait until the end to do everything!

Self explanatory, you understand the consequences!

2.

Use JSTOR and take notes about your sources!

This person reported that they wasted a lot of time rereading sources because they forgot about what they actually included. They also suggested using JSTOR and utilizing specific sources (including quotes ("), time ranges, etc.). Chemistry

3.

Read for understanding, not verbatim.

This person suggested reading the introductions and conclusions of each article so that they can form a more clear understanding of the experiment. The middle of the paper often contains gobs of scientific jargon and extra details that are likely to obscure the relevant details of the paper.

Lastly, take it step by step! Thinking about the paper in terms of individual goals will make it much more manageable. This is a life skill that is sure to benefit you no matter what you do in your life.

4. Chemistry

"How does an increase in the number of carbon atoms in primary alcohols affect the yield and purity of their respective esters prepared using propanoic acid?"

This is an example of an experimental science paper. Your advisor will help you a lot with the overall design of the equipment. The school has a variety of chemicals available and in some cases they will be willing to purchase chemicals on your behalf.

1.

Be prepared to do a complete 180 on your question, formulating a good question is one of the hardest parts of the whole paper!

2.

Allocate more time than you think you need for data collection, your first experiement iteratiors will likely be used to work out kinks in the process.

3

Record everything and take loads of pictures, this will prove invaluable at the end of the experiment.

4

Refer to Chemsitry IAs and the Textbook for information and for what IB is expecting regarding quantitative error analysis (like propagation of uncertainties, IAs will often be a good source of this type of analysis).

It should be noted that investigating a complex topic is less important than your thoroughness and critical reflection (ie. analyzing errors, suggesting improvements, proposing original and meaningful extensions, etc.).

5. Technology in a Global Society

"To what extent is semantic search more effective, in terms of finding information to meet one’s needs, than keyword search under the conditions of the countries with high, moderate, and low censorship?"

This essay was composed of two different parts: comparing the two searching algorithms as well as examining the effect of governmental censorship on the internet.

1.

Plan early!

Like I discussed in my personal Extended Essay, robust planning pays dividends. This person found that they needed to find a dependable VPN to connect to countries with high censorship as well as confounding variables such as search engine choice, browser choice, query selection, etc.

2

Understand the limitations of your exploration.

This person found out that they there was a number of flaws in their experiment including language and cultural differences of each country. Since there often isn’t an easy solution to this problem, they suggest identifying and explaining these limitations in your paper.

6. Math

"How is the theory of elliptic curves used in the encryption and decryption of data?"

This paper seems to be a more exploratory paper rather than an experimental paper. IB separates different disciplines, especially sciences, into experimental and non-experimental. For math, they do not require an "experiment", but for computer science they do. Mathematics papers are particularly good for those who want to prioritize their learning because you can just learn a sub branch of math and then talk about it in your EE.

1.

Have a prewrite to plan all of the subtopics you’re covering so you don’t over-research for your paper.

2.

Fully research your topic before writing unless you are fully confident in your knowledge.

3.

Write your rough draft early, leave the EE alone for a few weeks, and then reread! This is an especialy good piece of advice because it allows for peer review during this time and a fresh outlook on the paper when you return.

4.

If it takes more than a page or two to explain a concept, you should consider replacing it with something easier.

7. Resources

-

Scientific Articles and Textbooks: SciHub and Library Genesis are two questionably legal websites that provide free access to textbooks and scientific articles.

-

Learning Resources: MITOCW (MIT Open Courseware) is the cream of the crop when it comes to learning any university level STEM subject.

-

Coding: There is no one resource that fully encompasses everything you need to know about coding. Most students, whether they are familiar with coding or not will benefit from learning the R language, which is a statistical programming language. It can generate graphics which are visually pleasing and it will simplify any statisitical tests you may have to run.

-

Bibliography: Please use Bibguru.com. All of the other software provided to you, partially inclusive of NoodleTools, is either slow, doesn’t load on your personal computer, or doesn’t export to BibTex format (the format required if you are using LATEX). Bibguru has very few ads and runs well on Chromebooks.

-

Word Processing (making your document look good): LATEX (pronounced Lay-Tek) is the professional standard. It may look intimidating, especially if you are not familiar with typesetting languages but it will make formatting your document much easier. In addition, it automatically adjusts you citations (automatically switchs formats depending on style, removes citations not inline cited from the end of paper references, etc.). You can compile (process the documents) locally on your computer but for people not familiar with type-setting software, Overleaf is a great website to get you started. On there they have a great written introduction to LATEX. As for videos, Luke Smith has a great introduction however his delivery can be seen as unprofessional.

-

Other Help: I personally did not use the following resources but they would’ve been a great help for any of my questions. IB has an Discord and a Reddit page where IB grads and current students will gladly answer any of your questions. In addition, if you are doing a computer science EE, I would be more than happy to help. Feel free to email me at: me@iannbridges.com!
